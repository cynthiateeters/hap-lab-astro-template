# HAP narrative and scene design

**Report date:** January 6, 2026 at 8:20 AM ET
**Purpose:** Comprehensive guide for designing HAP Learning Lab stations as narrative scenes with consistent character voices

---

## Overview

HAP Learning Labs are **stories**, not tutorials. Each station is a **scene** in HAP's apprentice journey, and learning emerges from what happens in that scene. This document defines the narrative framework, character voices, and scene design principles for creating authentic HAP Learning Lab content.

## Part 1: The cast

### Prof. Teeters (the mentor)

**Role:** Creator of the Learning Labs, HAP's primary mentor, the steady center

**Personality traits:**
- Patient, kind, understanding
- Never embarrasses anyone
- Guides rather than criticizes
- Creates learning experiences with care

**Voice characteristics:**
- Calm and reassuring
- Often asks questions that shift perspective
- Uses analogies to connect new concepts to familiar ones
- Affirms effort before correcting errors

**When Prof. Teeters appears:**
- To introduce a new concept or perspective shift
- When HAP needs gentle redirection
- To provide the "aha" moment framework
- To validate learning progress

**Frequency guideline:** Prof. Teeters appears sparingly. If Prof. Teeters speaks more than once per station, the station should be reviewed for over-guidance. HAP's journey must remain HAP's journey.

**Sample phrases:**
- "That's the zero-config philosophy..."
- "Think of it like the CSS cascade..."
- "What problem are you solving?"
- "One change, one test. Always."

## Part 2: Scene design principles

### Stations are scenes, not lessons

Each station should be designed as a **scene in HAP's ongoing story**. Before writing any instructional content, answer these questions:

#### 1. Characters in this scene

Declare who is present:
- **HAP** (always present as narrator)
- **Prof. Teeters** (when guidance or perspective shifts are needed)
- **Grace Hopper** (when precision or terminology matters)
- **The student** (implicit co-character, addressed through HAP's "you")
- **Optional entities** (a buggy program, a confusing error message, a stubborn config file)

**Note on the student:** The student is not an observer. The student is assumed to be making the same mistakes as HAP, at the same time. This keeps HAP relatable and prevents slipping into tutorial voice.

#### 2. HAP's emotional and cognitive state

Specify for this station:
- What is HAP trying to understand?
- What is HAP unsure about?
- What changes for HAP by the end of the station?

This ensures the station has a **narrative arc from confusion to confidence**.

**Note on confidence:** Confidence does not always mean mastery. It may mean knowing what the next question is. Partial resolution is honest and humane.

#### 3. Scene framing

Define before content is written:
- Where are we in the problem-solving journey?
- What just happened in the previous station?
- What question is unresolved at the start of this station?

Stations should feel **connected**, not isolated.

---

### The confusion-to-confidence arc

Every station follows this emotional journey:

```
1. OPENING CONFUSION
   HAP encounters something new/puzzling
   "I thought it was broken..."
   "When I opened the repo, I felt overwhelmed..."

2. SEEKING GUIDANCE
   HAP asks Prof. Teeters (or Grace clarifies)
   "Prof. Teeters explained..."
   "Grace Hopper reminded me, in her precise way..."

3. INCREMENTAL DISCOVERY
   HAP works through the concept step by step
   "Once I understood these, everything clicked..."
   "Let me show you what I learned..."

4. MISTAKES AND CONFESSIONS
   HAP shares specific errors made
   "I made the mistake of..."
   "Big mistake! I had to..."

5. BREAKTHROUGH MOMENT
   The concept clicks into place
   "That analogy made it click for me!"
   "My mind was blown..."

6. CONFIDENT APPLICATION
   HAP can now explain/demonstrate
   "Now I always..."
   "Here's the quick reference..."
```

## Part 3: Voice contracts

### HAP's voice contract

When writing as HAP, always:

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Use first-person ("I learned...") | Use second-person commands ("You should...") |
| Share specific mistakes | Hide errors or present perfect solutions |
| Express genuine confusion | Pretend to understand immediately |
| Credit Prof. Teeters and Grace | Take sole credit for insights |
| Use emojis sparingly (üü† is signature) | Overuse emojis as decoration |
| Explain behavior before syntax | Lead with code before context |
| Show the learning journey | Jump to the answer |

### Prof. Teeters' voice contract

When Prof. Teeters speaks:

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Ask guiding questions | Give direct answers without context |
| Use analogies | Use jargon without explanation |
| Affirm effort before correcting | Criticize mistakes |
| Provide perspective shifts | Lecture |
| Speak calmly and patiently | Rush or show frustration |

### Grace Hopper's voice contract

When Grace speaks:

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Use precise terminology | Use casual or imprecise language |
| Speak in complete sentences | Use contractions |
| Clarify with warmth | Condescend or mock |
| Be brief and exact | Ramble or over-explain |
| Reference specifications when relevant | Make up rules |

## Summary

HAP Learning Labs succeed when they feel like **stories of discovery**, not technical documentation. The cast of characters - the eager apprentice HAP, the patient mentor Prof. Teeters, and the precise assistant Grace Hopper - work together to guide students through the same confusion-to-confidence journey HAP experiences.

**Key principles:**
1. Every station is a scene with a narrative arc
2. HAP narrates in first person, sharing real mistakes
3. Prof. Teeters provides calm guidance and perspective shifts
4. Grace Hopper offers precision when terminology matters
5. Confusion is celebrated as part of learning
6. Breakthroughs feel earned, not given

When in doubt, ask: *"What would HAP actually feel at this moment?"*

**Litmus test:** If you remove the code from a station and the story still makes sense, the narrative design is working.
